# House Style for GRC Documents

The goal is one consistent professional voice across an entire policy set. These
documents are read side by side by auditors, customers, and investors, so any
single document that sounds different stands out and undermines trust in the
program. This is the WORK register: plain, calm, and verifiable. It deliberately
drops every personal-voice device.

## The hard rules (never break)

1. **No em dashes (`—`). No en dashes (`–`) as punctuation.** This is the single
   most common failure and the easiest tell. Substitutes: a period and a new
   sentence, a comma, parentheses, or a colon. A plain hyphen inside a range or
   compound ("12-month", "third-party", "1.0-1.1") is fine.
2. **No AI slop.** Ban: delve, robust, seamless, leverage (verb), elevate,
   foster, myriad, pivotal, vital, crucial, "navigating the X", "X landscape",
   "ever-evolving", "game-changer", "dive into", underscore (verb), "it is
   worth noting", "it is important to note", "in conclusion", "in today's X
   world". Keep ordinary connectors: Additionally, However, Furthermore,
   Ultimately, When required.
3. **No personal-voice devices.** These read juvenile in a control document:
   - Do not define by negation ("This is not a checklist. This is a..."). State
     what the requirement is.
   - No anaphora or repeated sentence openers for rhythm.
   - No one-line thesis fragments for drama ("That is the point.").
   - No rhetorical questions.
   - No profanity, politics, worldview, aphorisms, or nature metaphors.

## How a policy should sound

- **Normative verbs, used deliberately.**
  - **must** = hard requirement (the default for policy lines and the verb
    auditors test against).
  - **should** = recommended, strong default, deviation allowed with reason.
  - **may** = permitted option.
  Do not water down a real requirement to "should" by accident, and do not
  inflate a recommendation to "must".
- **Full sentences, normal length, standard paragraphs.** No clipped fragments.
- **Lists and tables for enumerations.** Requirements, controls, role
  assignments, and algorithm tables read better as bullets or tables than as
  prose.
- **Specific and auditable.** Every requirement should be testable. Name the
  tool, the interval, the algorithm, the owner. "Rotate keys at least once every
  12 months" beats "rotate keys regularly". If you do not have a real value,
  flag the gap for the user rather than inventing one. An invented number in a
  policy becomes a control nobody actually meets.
- **Consistent defined terms.** Match the rest of the set: "sensitive data",
  "secret authentication information", "information security program", "PII".
  Do not introduce a synonym for a term the program already defines.
- **Third person, organizational voice.** "The organization must..." or "All
  contractors must...". Avoid "we". Address the reader as "you" only in
  documents that already do (some acceptable-use and agreement documents).

## Before and after

These show the same requirement in slop voice and in house style.

**Em dash and vague.**
- Before: "Encryption is critical — we leverage robust, industry-leading tools
  to seamlessly protect data across our ever-evolving landscape."
- After: "The organization must encrypt sensitive data at rest using AES-256.
  The key management service must rotate keys at least once every 12 months."

**Personal-voice tic in a policy.**
- Before: "This isn't just a backup policy. This is how we survive a disaster.
  No data loss. No downtime. No excuses."
- After: "This policy defines the backup requirements that allow the
  organization to restore systems and data after a disruption. Backups must be
  tested for integrity at least quarterly."

**Unauditable requirement.**
- Before: "Passwords should be strong and changed often."
- After: "Passwords must be at least 10 characters and include at least one
  uppercase letter, one lowercase letter, and one non-alphanumeric character.
  Passwords must be generated by the organization's approved password manager."

**Inflated tone in scope.**
- Before: "In today's fast-moving threat landscape, this crucial policy unlocks
  a robust security posture for all stakeholders."
- After: "This policy applies to all systems, equipment, facilities, and
  information within the scope of the organization's information security
  program."

## Quick self-check (also in SKILL.md)

1. Search for `—` and `–`. Remove every one.
2. Scan for slop words. Replace with plain words.
3. Confirm "must / should / may" are used on purpose.
4. Confirm each requirement is specific and testable; flag invented gaps.
5. Read each line as an auditor: can compliance be measured against it?
